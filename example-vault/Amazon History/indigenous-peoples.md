Long before Francisco de Orellana descended the Amazon in 1541, the river basin was home to **millions of people** organized into complex, sophisticated societies. The story of the Amazon's indigenous peoples is one of the oldest and most consequential on the continent — and one still unfolding.

## Pre-Contact Civilizations

^marajoara

Archaeological evidence shows that the Amazon was far more densely populated before European contact than previously understood. The **Marajoara culture**, centered on Marajó Island at the mouth of the Amazon, created elaborate polychrome ceramics, earthen mounds, and complex social hierarchies as early as 400 CE. Their pottery rivals the finest pre-Columbian work anywhere in the Americas.

Deeper in the basin, pre-contact peoples constructed the **terra preta** (dark earth) — a man-made soil of exceptional fertility, created by mixing charcoal, bone, and organic matter into the naturally poor Amazonian earth. These soils are found across millions of hectares and represent a millennia-long project of landscape management. The Amazon was not a pristine wilderness — it was a garden, cultivated at continental scale.

^orellana-contact

When Orellana's expedition descended the river in 1541–1542, his chronicler Gaspar de Carvajal described **vast cities stretching for miles along the riverbanks**, with roads, plazas, and populations that astonished the Europeans. These accounts were dismissed for centuries as fantasy — and then, beginning in the 1990s, confirmed by lidar surveys and systematic archaeological excavation.

## The People of the Rivers

The Amazon basin was home to hundreds of distinct peoples, each with their own language, cosmology, and territorial relationship with the forest. Some major groups of the Amazonas region:

- **Tikuna** — The most populous indigenous nation in Brazil today, living along the upper Solimões River
- **Yanomami** — Inhabitants of the highlands between Brazil and Venezuela; their forest knowledge remains unmatched
- **Mura** — River-dwelling people who resisted Portuguese colonization for over a century
- **Manaós** — The people who gave their name to the city of Manaus; nearly decimated by colonial-era conflicts

## Colonization and Catastrophe

^jesuit-missions

European contact brought catastrophe at a scale that is still difficult to comprehend. **Smallpox, measles, influenza** — diseases to which Amazonian peoples had no immunity — swept through communities faster than the Europeans themselves could travel. Entire nations were erased before any European had laid eyes on them, the disease spreading through existing trade networks.

The Portuguese Jesuit missions, beginning in earnest in the early 17th century, gathered survivors into *aldeamentos* (mission villages) — ostensibly for protection and conversion, in practice for labor extraction. Indigenous peoples were taught Portuguese, forced to abandon their languages, and put to work harvesting *drogas do sertão* (forest products) for the colonial economy.

^pombal

In 1755, the Marquis of Pombal expelled the Jesuits from Brazil and formally granted indigenous peoples legal freedom. In practice, the disruption of the missions left many communities without protection, subject to outright enslavement by secular colonists.

## The Cabanagem

^cabanagem

Between 1835 and 1840, the Amazon region was convulsed by the **Cabanagem** — a bloody popular uprising of indigenous peoples, mixed-race *caboclos*, and the rural poor against the colonial elite. Centered in Belém but spreading throughout the Amazon basin, the rebellion was crushed with extreme violence by Brazilian and Portuguese forces.

The death toll is estimated at **30,000 to 40,000 people** — roughly **30 to 40 percent** of the entire population of the Amazon region at the time, making it one of the deadliest conflicts in the history of the Americas. The indigenous population of the Amazon basin never fully recovered demographically from the combined effects of disease, slavery, and the Cabanagem.

## Survival and Recognition

^spi

The 20th century brought a slow, incomplete shift in Brazilian state policy toward indigenous peoples. The **Indian Protection Service (SPI)**, created in 1910 by Marshal Cândido Rondon, established the principle that indigenous peoples should be protected rather than destroyed — though protection often meant forced assimilation.

^constitution

The **1988 Brazilian Constitution** marked a genuine turning point. For the first time, the constitution explicitly recognized indigenous peoples' rights to their lands, cultures, and social organization. Article 231 guaranteed permanent possession of their traditional territories.

Today, Amazonas is home to **more than 60 distinct indigenous peoples**, many living in demarcated territories that together cover a significant portion of the state. The Amazon's indigenous communities remain at the center of ongoing disputes over land, mining, logging, and the future of the forest itself.

> "The forest is not a resource. The forest is our home, and we are part of it."
> — Davi Kopenawa, Yanomami shaman and activist
