The **Porto Flutuante de Manaus** (Floating Port of Manaus) is one of the great engineering achievements of the Belle Époque Amazon. Inaugurated in 1906 and designed by the Scottish firm **Sunderland Forge**, the floating dock system solved one of the Amazon's most punishing practical problems: the river rises and falls by up to **14 meters** between dry and wet seasons.

A fixed dock would be underwater for months, then perched on dry land — useless in either condition. The solution was radical: build a dock that floats.

## Engineering

The floating section — approximately **150 meters long** — is connected to the shore by a long gangway that pivots and articulates as the river rises and falls. The entire structure rises and falls with the Amazon, maintaining a constant relationship with the waterline regardless of season.

The dock was made from **English steel**, transported in pieces up the Amazon and assembled on site. It is thought to be one of the oldest operational floating docks in the world.

> At the peak of the rubber boom, up to 30 oceangoing vessels docked here simultaneously, loading rubber bales for Europe and North America.

The floating port remains in active commercial use today — over 100 years after its inauguration — and is considered a Brazilian **National Heritage Site**.
