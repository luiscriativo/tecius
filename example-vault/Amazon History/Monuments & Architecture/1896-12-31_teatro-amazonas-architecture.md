The **Teatro Amazonas** is simultaneously a work of architecture, a statement of colonial ambition, and an act of defiance against geography. That it exists at all — completed in 1896, in a city reachable only by river, surrounded by thousands of kilometers of rainforest — is extraordinary.

## Construction

Construction began in 1884, was interrupted multiple times by funding crises and the death of the original architect, and was finally completed in 1896 under Italian engineer **Celestial Sacardim** with decorative work by **Crispim do Amaral**.

Materials arrived by ship from Europe, were transferred at Belém to river steamers, and then carried upriver for weeks to Manaus. The journey alone for some pieces — particularly the marble columns and iron structural members — took months.

## Architectural Details

**The Dome**
The theater's most iconic feature is its **ribbed dome**, rising 36 meters above the stage. It is covered in **36,000 ceramic tiles** hand-painted in the colors of the Brazilian flag — green, gold, and blue — imported from the Alsace region of France. In the equatorial sunlight, the dome catches fire with color. It is visible from the river.

**The Interior**
- Seating for **701 spectators** across four tiers of boxes and a main stall
- **Carrara marble** columns, staircases, and decorative elements
- **Crystal chandeliers** from Murano, Venice
- Ceiling fresco titled *A Glorificação das Belas Artes no Amazonas* (The Glorification of Fine Arts in Amazonas), painted in Paris
- **Rubber-lined floor** in the entrance hall — so that arriving carriages made no noise

**The Exterior**
- Façade in **Italian Renaissance** style, painted in ochre and white
- Colonnade of slender columns facing the *Praça São Sebastião*
- Iron decorative elements from Scotland
- Grand entrance stairs in Portuguese marble

## Decline and Restoration

With the collapse of the rubber boom, the Teatro Amazonas closed in 1924. For decades it stood empty, slowly deteriorating. A first restoration in 1929 allowed limited use. The major restoration completed in **1990** brought the theater fully back to life.

Today the Teatro Amazonas is the home of the **Amazonas Philharmonic Orchestra** and hosts the **Amazonas Opera Festival** each year. It was the backdrop for the opening of Werner Herzog's film *Fitzcarraldo* (1982) — a film that is itself about the mad dream of bringing opera to the Amazon.
