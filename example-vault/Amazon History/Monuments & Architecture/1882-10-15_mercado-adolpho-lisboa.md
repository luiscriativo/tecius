Inaugurated in 1882 and named after a former state governor, the **Mercado Municipal Adolpho Lisboa** is one of the finest examples of 19th-century iron architecture in South America — and, improbably, stands in the middle of the Amazon jungle.

The market was modeled on the famous **Les Halles** market in Paris, designed with cast-iron columns, wrought-iron latticework, and glazed tile panels that allowed natural light to flood the interior. All structural iron was imported from England; the decorative azulejo tiles came from Portugal.

> The market was — and remains — a working food and craft market. This is not a museum piece. The same stalls that sold forest products to rubber barons now sell *tucumã*, *cupuaçu*, and river fish to Manaus residents.

## Architecture

- **Style:** Art Nouveau with iron structure
- **Materials:** English cast iron, Portuguese azulejo tiles, tropical hardwood
- **Features:** Central hall with glass-and-iron roof, riverside arcade, decorative ironwork along every cornice
- **Area:** Approximately 3,000 m²

The market underwent major restoration in 2018 and remains fully operational, one of Manaus's most visited historic sites and a living piece of the Belle Époque that built the city.
