By 1912, the Amazon rubber boom was over. British-grown rubber from Southeast Asian plantations — particularly in Malaya and Ceylon — flooded world markets at a fraction of the price Amazonian *seringueiros* could compete with. The cause traced back to a single act of **botanical espionage**.

In 1876, British explorer **Henry Wickham** had smuggled approximately 70,000 *Hevea brasiliensis* seeds out of Brazil — an act that Brazil considered theft, Britain framed as science. Kew Gardens cultivated them. The British Empire planted them across its Southeast Asian colonies. By 1913, plantation rubber from Asia produced at costs that made wild-harvested Amazonian rubber economically unviable.

## The Aftermath

- Rubber prices collapsed from **12 shillings per pound** (1910) to under 2 shillings by 1913
- Manaus lost nearly a third of its population within a decade
- The Teatro Amazonas closed and fell into disrepair
- Wealthy rubber barons were ruined almost overnight
- Thousands of *seringueiros* were abandoned deep in the forest without pay or passage home

The Amazon would not see significant economic recovery until the Free Trade Zone policy of 1967.
