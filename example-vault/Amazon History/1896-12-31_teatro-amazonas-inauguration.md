On December 31, 1896, the **Teatro Amazonas** — the Amazon Opera House — was inaugurated in Manaus, standing as the most audacious symbol of the rubber boom era. Built over seventeen years at staggering cost, it remains one of the most remarkable buildings in South America.

The theater was constructed almost entirely from **imported European materials**: Carrara marble from Italy, steel from England, ceramic tiles from Alsace (painted in the colors of the Brazilian flag), and a magnificent dome covered in **36,000 painted ceramic tiles** imported from Portugal, shimmering in the equatorial sun in shades of green, gold, and blue.

> To build an opera house in the middle of the Amazon jungle, at a time when Manaus had no roads, no railways, and was reachable only by river — this was either an act of supreme civilization or supreme madness. Perhaps both.

## Key Facts

- **Capacity:** 701 seats
- **Architects:** Celestial Sacardim (original design), Crispim do Amaral (renovation)
- **Total cost:** Over £2 million in 1896 values
- **Dome tiles:** 36,000 hand-painted ceramic tiles from Alsace
- **Flooring:** Amazon rubber was used to line the floor, allowing carriages to arrive in silence

The Teatro Amazonas hosted European opera companies, orchestras, and theater troupes who made the weeks-long journey up the Amazon to perform. It stood as proof — at least to its builders — that civilization had arrived in the jungle.
