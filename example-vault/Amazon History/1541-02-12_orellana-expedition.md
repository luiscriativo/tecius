On February 12, 1541, Spanish conquistador **Francisco de Orellana** departed from Quito with a small crew of around fifty men, venturing east into the unknown. What began as a supply mission into the Amazon jungle became the **first European navigation of the entire Amazon River**.

After months drifting downstream, Orellana and his crew reached the Atlantic Ocean in August 1542 — having covered nearly **6,400 kilometers** through dense rainforest, navigating treacherous currents and hostile encounters with riverside communities.

> "We saw on the banks of the river very large cities that glistened in white and were as big as Seville."
> — Gaspar de Carvajal, expedition chronicler

## Legacy

- Orellana named the river *Amazonas* after warrior women he claimed to have encountered
- The expedition's account described **dense, populous settlements** along the riverbanks — civilizations later dismissed as myth, and only recently confirmed by archaeology
- His route established Spain's awareness of the Amazon basin's scale
- The journey inspired two centuries of European expeditions into the interior
