In 1858, the Brazilian government opened the Amazon River to **international steamship navigation**, ending centuries of Portuguese-era restrictions on foreign vessels. The American entrepreneur **Irineu Evangelista de Sousa** (later Baron of Mauá) had been granted a monopoly to operate the first steamship line on the river years earlier, in 1853.

The arrival of regular steam-powered vessels transformed the Amazon economy. Manaus — previously reachable only after weeks by canoe from Belém — was now connected to Atlantic trade routes with a journey measured in days.

This opening was the infrastructure prerequisite for the rubber boom that would follow within two decades. Without reliable river transport, Amazonian rubber could not have reached world markets at the scale that would make Manaus one of the wealthiest cities in South America.
